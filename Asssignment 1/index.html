01. What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

- I initially drew my eyes to the main typography. The large, bold text dominated the screen, serving as the visual anchor of the homepage.

02. Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

- Over two minutes, I moved through three main pathways. First, I clicked "Explore Projects," which took me to the projects page. I then scrolled down and tapped into an individual project subpage. Second, I clicked "See my Resume →" to open a PDF file. Third, I scrolled down the homepage, clicked a sidebar element, and was taken to an external Awwwards listing of the portfolio.

03. What role did color play in your interaction with the page?

- I spent the most time on the projects section at minjeaseo.com/projects. Skimming through the case studies and navigating between individual project pages required more attention than other parts of the site.

04. What was the most common action in your two-minute interaction with the experience?

- Clicking was by far my most repeated action during the two-minute experience. I moved between pages, opened subpages, and followed sidebar links, which made clicking the dominant mode of interacting with this site rather than scrolling or reading.

05. What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

- In personal opinion, the website aimed to present the work experience in a clean, professional context, but with minimalistic execution to the point it feels underwhelming, especially for a designer’s portfolio. These restraints suggested an intention to let the work speak for itself, though this did not fully come through.

06. How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

- The web communicates its goal through modest visual language: monotone (mainly) colors, minimal illustration and a clean layout. By removing decorative noise, the design directs attention toward the project content itself, despite the lack of personality that makes it hard to sense the designer’s identity and brand.

07. What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

- The portfolio reads as an infrequently visited site for recruiters and collaborators to browse briefly during evaluation. However, given that this is the work of a senior designer, there was a missed opportunity to build a more immersive experience through richer visuals that reflects their personality.

08. How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

- There is consistency in how elements - header, a sidebar, and a box-based project grid - to signal the web’s navigational logic. These familiar UI patterns make it immediately clear how to move through the content, suggesting a linear browsing experience than one designed for repeated or deep engagement.

09. What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

- The experience references two other media forms of external web pages embedded or linked through project images, and a printable PDF resume hosted on Google Drive. These extensions pull the user outside the portfolio environment and into more functional and communicative formats.

10. What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

- The linked external pages and PDF suggest users should treat this as a starting point for research, not a complete picture. The references invite users to investigate the work more deeply by visiting live sites or downloading documentation, signaling that active exploration rather than passive reading are expected.

11. What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

- The inclusion of a polished PDF resume and cleanly formatted external links and websites communicates a sense of professionalism and credibility. However, the sparse visual environment makes the experience feel neutral and detached to me.

12. What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

- The most frustrating element was the repeated use of the same layout format across both the featured projects section on the homepage and the related projects section within individual subpages. Because the visual structure was identical, it became difficult to know which page I was on, disrupting the sense of navigation and making the experience feel repetitive.

13. What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

- The minimalistic design and concise information architecture made browsing feel effortless. Content was easy to locate and digest without feeling overwhelmed, which created a calm interaction, which is something often harder to achieve than it looks in portfolio design.
